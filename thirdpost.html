Unemployment: The Countercyclical Coin of Economic Growth

Jobless Recoveries: When Economic Growth Leaves Unemployment Behind

Posted by Noah Gaw on April 28, 2023

According to calculations made this year, the Fed expects the unemployment rate to increase to 4.4% next year from 3.7% in August as they increase borrowing prices and slow the economy. According to experts, such a rise in the unemployment rate over the course of a year would probably result in the loss of more than 1 million jobs and the inability of millions more people to find better employment opportunities or higher-paying positions.

A statistic that typically changes by just 0.1 point, if at all, from month to month, the labor force participation rates the percentage of Americans who are in the labor force rose by 0.3 percentage points to 62.4%.

On the contrary, the job market is strong, with continuous job growth and plenty of openings, so employees won't likely experience prolonged unemployment. Additionally, it's excellent news for companies who are having problems filling positions because they now have a larger pool of candidates to pick from.

"Unemployment is not a problem that can be solved by simply creating more jobs. It's a problem that requires a comprehensive approach that includes education, training, and support for workers." - Nancy Pelosi

The quote by Nancy Pelosi suggests that solving the issue of unemployment is not as simple as just creating more job opportunities. Instead, it requires a multifaceted approach that includes education and training programs to improve workers' skills and support services to help them find and maintain employment. In other words, addressing unemployment is not just about increasing the number of jobs available, but also about equipping workers with the necessary skills and resources to fill those jobs.

Jobless Recoveries: When Economic Growth Leaves Unemployment Behind

The article discusses the relationship between economic growth and unemployment, which can be described as countercyclical, meaning that unemployment rises when economic growth is low and vice versa. The article also notes that unemployment is a complex issue that cannot be solved simply by creating more jobs, but rather requires a comprehensive approach that includes education, training, and support for workers. Additionally, the article discusses the natural rate of unemployment and how it can be influenced by labor market conditions and policies. The article concludes by highlighting the importance of accurately measuring unemployment and the potential for hidden unemployment in government statistics.

Students are looking for job in China

China’s 19.6% youth unemployment rate is nearing a record-high and may pose ‘a threat to social stability’

Despite an improving overall economy, the youth unemployment situation in China remains high, according to Alicia Garcia-Herrero, chief Asia Pacific economist at Natixis. The National Bureau of Statistics reported better-than-expected 4.5% GDP growth in the first quarter, and retail sales and exports also showed strong growth. However, urban joblessness for young people remains high, with 5.3% in March, compared to the overall rate of 4.5%.
